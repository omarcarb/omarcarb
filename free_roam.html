Market Research

What The Research Shows

In 2017, 13.9% of people with disabilities drove / traveled regularly (down from 17.5% in 2009), a concerning pattern that restricts their mobility, access to essential services, and employment opportunities due to the constraints posed by their disabilities, underscoring the pressing need for enhanced para transit services and improved accessibility.

What The Research Concludes

People with mobility issues tend to travel less due to a lack of available resources such as public transit or no access to paratransit services.

Competitive Analysis

The first step in my design process was to look at how the competition approached the problem. I chose 3 competitors as my main inspiration

myRTCpara
Google Maps
Uber

What These Apps Do Right:

Google Maps offers accessibility information about a specific location

Uber allows for specific pick up instructions

myRTCpara offers paratransit services in a dedicated app

What These Apps Do Wrong:

Google Maps doesn’t offer transport alternatives

Uber doesn’t offer paratransit services

myRTCpara is local and their services are not available everywhere

Conclusion

Competitors offer some accessibility features but don’t offer much in the way of delivering a service that allows individuals with disabilities to move around their city.

Competitors that do offer a service for people with disabilities, they are all local services with no nationwide support. If a user were to travel abroad, they would lose that service.

Accommodations are a last consideration for most of the competitors. Those that do offer accommodations are services that specialize in those field but are local.

Grid Alignment and Heirarchy

Of course, choices and pretty colors aren’t everything to a design, it all has to flow perfectly as well. As such, I took into heavy consideration how each element aligns itself in an 8 point grid, with distances between groups or elements being anywhere between 8, 16, 24, or 32.

Prototype Validation

It was time to begin testing to see if the designs and the flows work well together. I gathered around 4 friends to help me test the app and give their opinions about its flow and how it looks. To eliminate bias, I explicitly told them that the app was made by an undisclosed individual with no relation to me.

All individuals were ask to participate in a simple 40 - 50 minute exercise where they had to complete a series of apps. The tasks ranged from asking the user to book a paratransit ride to a given location, to asking the users to find the settings menu. These tests were specifically designed to test for app usability and look for any issues with the design, such as poor contrast.

Before

In this iteration, All rides were on display for everyone to pick and choose what ride they wanted. This presented a problem as users couldn't find their preferred ride method

After

After the revision, I implemented a tab system which would sort rides to whatever the user chooses. This makes picking a ride quicker and easier for the user.

Study Results

1 out 4 users mentioned that the app had a confusing navigation menu, claiming that some of the icons were not properly visible. 3 out of 4 users failed to complete the task of booking a paratransit ride because “they weren't sure which option was the paratransit option

New and Improved

To fix the first issue, I simply made the visuals on the navigation menu, more visible and added labels on the bottom with recognizable icons. To fix the second issue, I decided that instead of having all options available at once, I would instead layout my options in a tab menu for each type of transportation

Accessibility Evaluation

Since this app is primarily targeted towards individuals with mobility issues, accessibility was a major consideration when designing the project. Not only did the text have to be readable to those that are visually impaired, but the structure of the app needs to be smooth enough for people to use and screen readers to breeze through each stage in the app
