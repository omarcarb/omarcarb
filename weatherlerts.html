Weatherlerts

A personal weather app with real time weather alerts

Overview

Project Summary

Weatherlerts is a mobile app project. It's designed to deliver early warning alerts to the user of any upcoming disasters. The project time was between August 2022 and September 2022.

Problem That Needs To Be Solved

Weather seems like an easy problem to tackle but there are several things that need to be considered first:

1

News viewership is down across the board as alternative media sources take their place

2

Weather is constantly changing and people hate weatherpeople who are wrong

3

People want weather information on locations outside their current one.

The Solution

Design an app that delivers up to date notifications of weather events. The app must:

1

Provide up to date weather alerts, both big and small, directly to the user.

2

Show news stories related to their locations weather

3

Allow users to pick and choose which locations to focus on.

My Role

Wireframing, Prototyping, Ideating, Research

Process to Product

I approached the project with assumptions in mind: users sought swift access to pertinent information, preferably on their mobile devices. Thus, I adopted a mobile-first design strategy to accommodate smaller screens effectively. This approach also enabled the incorporation of features like pop-up notifications.

Process

Research

Find out how the competition solves this problem, if they have solved it at all.

Ideation

Come up with as many ideas as possible and pick the best one from the bunch

Testing

See if the ideas work in practice and keep testing until it works

Design

Finalize the process by coming up with the final look and feel of the product

Ideation

My aim was to create a neatly organized app that minimized information clutter while providing essential weather details. Many design decisions in the ideation phase were centered around prioritizing weather alerts before general information, based on the assumption that users typically prioritize alerts.

Testing

Results

I measured success based on the time on a task the user spent. Findings for the study can be summarized as such:

1

Users were frustrated as to why the home screen wasn't the map screen

2

Button layouts were confusing.

3

Information was not shared properly.

4

Visually impaired individuals had a tough time navigating.

What to Do

I had a bit of a struggle figuring out how to make the app easy to use. Finally, I decided to get rid of the starting screen that wasn't really needed, and I moved some buttons around to make them easier to find. This really helped make the app much easier to use.

Design

Laying the Foundation

My first design try didn't work well when tested, so I had to start over. I chose a simple 8-point grid layout and a clean look. Picking colors was a challenge, but I settled on a calming blue like the sky. For the text, I went with the Roboto font since it's commonly used in many designs.

Results and Takeaways

After fine-tuning the app to its final version, we received a lot more positive feedback. Everyone was able to complete their tasks quickly and without any problems.

Some things that I took away from completing this project are:

1

Users like it when they get the info they need right away on the first page. In weather apps, having lots of useful stuff on that first page keeps more people interested.

2

Unlike most apps, weather apps like this one tend to get clicked through more. People find it convenient to access all the info they want with just a few taps.

3

Enhancing the user experience requires further testing. Our discussions with project participants highlight their predominant use of weather apps for planning purposes. Thus, an intriguing concept could involve enabling the app to sync with their calendar and issue alerts regarding weather conditions that could potentially impact their plans.

In Conclusion

While the market for personalized weather apps isn't as big as I expected, there's room for innovation. Many existing apps lack an integrated alert system. By filling this gap and adding alerts, we can greatly improve emergency readiness and user engagement, making the app even more valuable.
